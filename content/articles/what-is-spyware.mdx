Spyware is software designed to monitor activity and collect information without clear, informed permission. It may track browsing habits, record keystrokes, collect credentials, or send device information back to a third party.

## Why Spyware Is Different

Not all malware has the same goal. Some breaks things, some locks files, and some steals quietly. Spyware belongs to that last category. Its main purpose is often surveillance or data collection rather than obvious destruction.

That can make it harder to notice because it may try to stay hidden.

## Signs Spyware Might Be Present

A device with spyware may feel slower, show unusual pop-ups, redirect searches, or behave strangely in the background. Battery life can drop, data usage may rise, and security settings might change without a good explanation.

None of those signs prove spyware on their own, but they are enough to justify checking further.

## How Spyware Gets Installed

Spyware can arrive through fake apps, deceptive browser extensions, bundled downloads, malicious links, or unsafe software sources. On phones, suspicious app permissions can also be a clue that something is collecting more data than it should.

The pattern is usually the same: it sneaks in under the appearance of something useful or harmless.

## How to Protect Yourself

Install software from trusted sources, keep systems updated, review extension and app permissions, and remove anything you do not recognize or no longer need. Good security software can help, but careful habits still matter.

If you think a device is infected, treat passwords stored or typed on that device as potentially exposed.

## Summary

Spyware is software that secretly collects information from a device or its user. It is dangerous because it tries to observe rather than announce itself. For the bigger picture, see [What Is Malware?](/blog/what-is-malware/) and [What Is Metadata?](/blog/what-is-metadata/).
